Mandated Reporter Responsibilities

As an ICHHS employee, you are legally required to report suspected abuse or neglect of children and/or vulnerable adults.

📄 Required Reading
ℹ️
Action Required: You must read both mandated reporter training documents before marking this module complete.
👶 Child Protection Mandated Reporter Training 👴 Vulnerable Adult Mandated Reporter Training
⚠️
Key Concept: Professionals who work with children and/or vulnerable adults must report abuse or neglect. The person with firsthand knowledge must file the report — it is not your job to determine whether maltreatment occurred.
1

Your Responsibilities

As an ICHHS employee, you are a mandated reporter. This means you must report suspected abuse or neglect of children and/or vulnerable adults.
🔄 Flip for details

Mandated Reporter Duties

  • You are legally obligated to report
  • The person with firsthand knowledge must file the report
  • You cannot delegate this responsibility
  • Failure to report has legal consequences
🔄 Flip back
2

Recognizing Maltreatment

Knowing the signs of abuse, neglect, and maltreatment is the first step to protecting vulnerable individuals.
🔄 Flip for details

Signs to Watch For

  • Unexplained physical injuries
  • Inconsistent explanations from caregivers
  • Signs of emotional or psychological abuse
  • Indicators of neglect (hygiene, hunger, isolation)
  • Behavioral changes or fearfulness
🔄 Flip back
3

Reporting Procedures

When you suspect maltreatment, you must act promptly. Understand who, when, and how to report.
🔄 Flip for details

How to Report

  • Report immediately upon suspicion — do not wait
  • Contact your supervisor or the appropriate intake line
  • Document your observations factually
  • You do not need proof to make a report
  • Discuss with your supervisor as needed
🔄 Flip back
❓

Knowledge Check — Mandated Reporter

Scenario: You are meeting with a family and notice a child with unexplained bruising. The caregiver's explanation seems inconsistent. What should you do?
✅ Correct! You are the person with firsthand knowledge. You must report suspected maltreatment — it's not your decision to determine whether it is or isn't abuse.
❌ Not quite. As the person with firsthand knowledge, you must report immediately. Waiting or consulting others first could delay protection for a child at risk.
Review both mandated reporter training documents before proceeding.

Client Communication & De-escalation

Building rapport, active listening, de-escalation strategies, and recognizing warning signs before situations escalate.

📄 Required Reading
ℹ️
Action Required: You must review the following document before marking this module complete.
📊 Violence Prevention Strategies Presentation
1

Building Rapport

Rapport is not about being liked — it's about establishing mutual trust, honesty, and respect even when you disagree.
🔄 Flip for details

Building Rapport

  • Show genuine interest in the person
  • Respect differences of opinion
  • Use the same language and terms they use
  • Match body language appropriately
  • People with rapport are less likely to become aggressive
🔄 Flip back
2

Active Listening

Listening is about gaining understanding — not just hearing words, but tone, body language, and what goes unsaid.
🔄 Flip for details

Active Listening Skills

  • Clear your mind and focus fully on the speaker
  • Listen to hear, not to solve — let them finish
  • Watch for non-verbal cues and inconsistencies
  • Listen for ideas, not just words
  • Be patient — pauses are normal
🔄 Flip back
3

De-escalation Strategies

De-escalation begins before you speak — your tone, posture, and presence all send messages.
🔄 Flip for key strategies

Top De-escalation Strategies

  • Act calm even if you're not
  • Lower your voice — never match their volume
  • Validate feelings: "I see where you're coming from"
  • Give personal space and remove the audience
  • Ask: "What would help you right now?"
  • Never say "calm down"
🔄 Flip back
4

Recognizing Escalation

Knowing warning signs early allows you to intervene before a situation becomes dangerous.
🔄 Flip for details

Warning Signs

  • Raised voice, pacing, clenched fists
  • Making threats or demands
  • Flushed skin, rapid breathing
  • Loss of humor, increased sarcasm
  • Moving toward you or others aggressively
  • When in doubt — seek assistance
🔄 Flip back
❓

Knowledge Check — De-escalation

Scenario: You're meeting with Arnie Williams and have to inform him that his benefits are being put on hold. Arnie becomes upset and begins raising his voice. What is the best first response?
✅ Correct! De-escalation techniques include lowering your voice and validating concerns. Matching their energy or telling them to calm down will escalate the situation further.
❌ Not quite. The best response is to stay calm, lower your voice, and acknowledge their concerns. Never say "calm down" or raise your voice in response.
Review the Violence Prevention Strategies document before proceeding.

🔄 Flip for details

Communicating with LEP Individuals

Effective communication requires cultural respect and confidentiality, even when using interpreter services.
🔄 Flip for details

LEP Communication Tips

  • Speak directly to the client, not the interpreter
  • Be patient and allow extra time
  • Maintain confidentiality through the interpreter
  • Be culturally respectful and sensitive

Knowledge Check — Language Access

Scenario: A new individual on your caseload walks in unexpectedly and, in broken English, tells you they don't understand a form you've asked them to complete. What should you do?
✅ Correct! Individuals must be offered interpreter services when needed. Services cannot be denied due to language barriers.
❌ Not quite. You must provide meaningful access to services. Offer interpreter services through Language Line — never ask clients to use family members for case-related matters or deny services due to language barriers.
